Acoustic Neuroma (acoo-stik nyor-oma)

A non-cancerous tumour that grows on the nerve used for hearing and balance.

How does it feel?

The symptoms of acoustic neuroma usually develop gradually and can include:
  • hearing loss
  • ringing or buzzing in the ear (tinnitus)
  • dizziness or spinning sensation (vertigo)
Hearing loss caused by acoustic neuroma usually affects only one ear. In addition, a large acoustic neuroma can also sometimes cause persistent headaches, blurred vision, facial numbness, problems with coordination, a hoarse voice or difficulty swallowing. Acoustic neuromas tend to affect adults and usually have no obvious cause, although a small number of cases are the result of a genetic condition called neurofibromatosis type 2 (NF2).

What can we do?

If your doctor thinks you may have an acoustic neuroma, you'll be referred to our clinic for further tests, such as an MRI or CT scan and hearing tests. Treatment depends on the size and position of your tumour, how fast it's growing and your general health.
We will discuss the options at your appointment which might include monitoring with regular brain scans, surgery to remove the tumour and stereotactic radiosurgery. Radiosurgery is treatment with a precise beam of radiation from outside of the body to stop the tumour getting any bigger All these options carry some risks and some benefits and we encourage you to talk openly about your wishes so that we can come to a shared plan at your appointment.

What about the future?

You'll need to have regular scans after treatment to check if the tumour is growing again. Most tumours grow very slowly and those that grow quickly are usually treated before they become too big. If growth happens quickly and remains untreated, however, complications can arise.
Even with treatment, symptoms such as hearing loss and tinnitus can persist and affect your ability to work, communicate or drive. We can offer more information and support to manage your symptoms at your appointment. There are a number of research trials underway to try and improve the treatment of acoustic neuroma, and you may be asked if you wish to take part in research at your appointment.
